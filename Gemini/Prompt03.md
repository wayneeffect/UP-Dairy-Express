Yes, your cousin is sitting on an absolute goldmine of an opportunity. Because he is already running the commercial routes to stores in the mid-to-eastern UP, **he has already solved the single hardest, most expensive part of a home delivery business: the logistics, the truck, the refrigeration, and the physical presence in those towns.**

Right now, he is driving a massive asset (his delivery truck) right past hundreds of homes in Newberry, Manistique, Engadine, and Naubinway. By launching a direct-to-consumer (D2C) and small-business delivery website, he can turn his existing windshield time into high-margin retail profit.

Here is how he can layer this onto his existing business, and exactly how much money he stands to make.

---

### The Logistical Advantage (Why his margins will be double anyone else's)

A normal home delivery startup fails in the UP because of fuel and vehicle wear-and-tear over vast distances. Your cousin completely bypasses this obstacle:

* **Zero Additional Fuel/Truck Capital:** He is already paying to drive from town to town to service his retail accounts. Stopping at a home or a small local office 2 minutes away from his grocery store stop costs him almost nothing extra in fuel.
* **Wholesale Sourcing Power:** He already has a direct relationship with the dairy supplier/processor. He is buying at wholesale or distributor pricing, meaning his gross margins on the product itself will be excellent.
* **The "While I'm There" Model:** He doesn't need to create a new route. He just drops residential orders off either right before or right after he services the local grocery store, gas station, or dollar store in that specific town.

---

### How Much Money Can He Make? (The Revenue Models)

Because this would be a side-stream of his main retail route, we will calculate this on **Incremental Net Profit** (money he keeps after the wholesale cost of goods, website processing fees, and extra time/labor).

Let's break it down by a realistic phase-one goal of securing **100 residential customers** and **15 small business/office accounts** across his entire mid-to-eastern UP footprint.

#### 1. Residential Home Delivery (The "Glass Bottle & Fresh" Appeal)

People in rural communities love the nostalgia and quality of farm-fresh delivery, especially families or elderly residents who want to avoid driving in nasty lake-effect snow.

* **Average Weekly Order Value (AOV):** $35 (e.g., 2 gallons of premium/organic milk, a roll of local butter, a pack of cheese curds, or local eggs).
* **Delivery Fee:** $5 per drop (completely standard for home delivery, and pure profit for him since he’s already in the town).
* **Total Weekly Revenue per House:** $40
* **Gross Margin:** ~45% (Since he buys at distributor pricing and has low extra fuel costs).
* **Weekly Profit per House:** ~$18
* **With 100 Homes:** **~$1,800 per week in net profit ($93,600 per year).**

#### 2. Small Business & Office Delivery (The "Coffee Station" Route)

Think about all the local banks, real estate offices, insurance agencies, motels, car dealerships, and school breakrooms in Manistique, Newberry, and surrounding areas. They use milk and creamers for their staff and customers daily, and someone usually has to run to the grocery store to buy it.

* **Average Weekly Order Value:** $50 (Multiple gallons, half-and-halfs, heavy creams, plus premium coffee additions).
* **Delivery Fee:** $5
* **Total Weekly Revenue per Business:** $55
* **Weekly Profit per Business:** ~$25
* **With 15 Small Businesses:** **~$375 per week in net profit ($19,500 per year).**

### Total Combined Potential Profit

By layering a home/office delivery website onto his existing commercial routes, your cousin could realistically add **$110,000+ per year in net profit** to his bottom line with just ~115 highly targeted accounts.

### How the Website Makes This Easy (No "Clipboard and Phone Call" Chaos)

To do this without losing his mind, your cousin shouldn't take phone calls or texts for orders. The website needs to do 100% of the administrative heavy lifting.

1. **Strict Routing by ZIP Code:** When a customer signs up on the website, they enter their address. If he only services Manistique on Tuesdays and Newberry on Thursdays, the website automatically assigns the customer to that specific day.
2. **Subscription Orders Only:** Customers set up a standing order (e.g., "2 gallons of whole milk every week" or "1 gallon of 2% bi-weekly"). The website automatically charges their credit card the night before delivery. He wakes up, prints a sheet generated by the website showing exactly what extra crates to pack into his truck for the day, and goes.
3. **Porch Cooler Rule:** To keep his delivery times lightning-fast, customers must leave an insulated cooler with ice packs on their porch. He pulls up, drops the milk in the cooler, and leaves. He doesn't knock, chat, or wait around—each stop takes less than 45 seconds.

Your cousin has a massive unfair competitive advantage. Anyone else trying to build this business from scratch in the mid-to-eastern UP would go broke on fuel costs alone. For him, it is an incredibly high-margin monetization of the empty space already sitting in his truck and the route miles he is already driving.
